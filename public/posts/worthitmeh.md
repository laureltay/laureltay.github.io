Four years ago, I turned down a competitive place in NUS dentistry to attend a small private university in upstate New York. It was the hardest decision I'd ever made, but also the one that changed my life the most.

Around that time, I was reading The Defining Decade by Meg Jay, a book about why your twenties are the most formative decade of your life, and how the choices you make in them shape everything that follows. One concept that stuck with me was _identity capital_: the collection of personal assets we build over time, the investments we make in ourselves that eventually become a part of who we are. Some of it is tangible: degrees, credentials, experience. But the more important kind is less visible: how you think, how you handle uncertainty, how you solve problems under pressure. Jay's advice for those in their 20s paralyzed by choice is simple: take the route that builds the most identity capital, the one that will teach and challenge you the most, not necessarily the safest or most straightforward one. That framing gave language to something I'd been feeling but couldn't properly articulate.

Growing up in Singapore, I think we were often subconsciously taught to value efficiency. The fastest route was the best one: DSA into the top secondary school, do IB to skip O-Levels, then straight into med/law/dent. Minimize risk, maximize opportunity. And to be fair, the system gave me a lot. It taught me discipline, drive, the ability to work hard and think critically. It taught me how to succeed. But somewhere along the way I started wondering if success and growth were actually the same thing. I realized I had consistently been asking _"what's the smartest path?"_ and rarely: _"what kind of person is this path turning me into?"_

Ultimately, I (fearfully) took that step of faith, trusting that the process of growth (and the identity capital it would build) would serve me in ways I couldn't yet see. And it did, more than I could have ever imagined.

I know the next four years will be a whole journey of its own. But more than anything, these past four years gave me immense clarity. It gave me the space to grow up, to make mistakes, to move across the world and build a life independently, to meet people who challenged the way I think. Somewhere along the way, I stopped pursuing medicine simply because it was a childhood dream or the “smart” path. I chose it again and again with full awareness of what it asks of me.
 
I know that after everything, It was worth every _“wah, so long, worth it meh?”_
