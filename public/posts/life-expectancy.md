Life expectancy at birth is one of those numbers that looks simple but isn't. It's a single figure meant to summarise the health of an entire nation, and in 2023, depending on where a baby happens to be born, it swings by more than thirty years. A child born in Japan can expect to live to about 85 while a child born in Nigeria, to about 55. The United States sits at 79 below the United Kingdom, and India (home to a sixth of humanity) lands almost exactly on the world average of 73. When looked at closely, life expectancy is really a measure of the conditions surrounding a person's life. Such as whether clean water is available, if vaccines reach children, whether there are enough doctors or if mothers survive childbirth, and if a child survives their first five years. So the story that is told here is not just about who lives longer where they are born, but about who has better/more access to basic systems that keep people alive.

This isn't a new idea. Decades of research on the *social determinants of health*, most influentially [Michael Marmot's work](https://discovery.ucl.ac.uk/676/1/Microsoft_Word_-_MM_Lancet_365_1099_UCL_eprints.pdf) and the [WHO Commission on Social Determinants of Health](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/WHO-IER-CSDH-08.1), show that the conditions people live in shape health more powerfully than medical care alone. Marmot's Whitehall studies found a steep gradient in mortality *even among British civil servants who all had the same access to the NHS*: it wasn't the healthcare that differed, it was everything around it.

P.S. I made the choice to leave out small city-states like Monaco and San Marino. Although they rank near the top, comparing Japan and Nigeria makes the inequality easier to see. > **What the chart hides**
>
> A national average is still an average. The same thirty-year gap that appears *between* countries also opens *within* them: between the rich and the poor, urban and rural communities, and different racial and ethnic groups. This chart flattens all of that into one dot per nation.
